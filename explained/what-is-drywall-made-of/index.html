What Is Drywall Made Of? (And How to Tell It Apart from Plaster)

June 14, 2026

The Short Answer

Drywall and plaster are the two most common interior wall materials in North American homes, but they require completely different repair approaches. Misidentifying your wall type leads to failed patches, wasted materials, and costly rework. I learned this the hard way when I tried patching a plaster wall in my first apartment with standard drywall mud — it cracked and fell out within a month. Use these inspection steps to confidently determine what your walls are made of before you open any repair materials.

What Drywall Actually Is

Drywall is a manufactured panel made of compressed gypsum plaster sandwiched between two layers of heavy paper. Standard panels are 1/2-inch thick, though 5/8-inch is used for fire-rated applications. The gypsum core is soft and chalky — you can dent it with moderate pressure. Drywall became the standard in North American homes after the 1970s because it’s faster, cheaper, and requires less skilled labor to install than traditional plaster.

What Plaster Actually Is

Traditional plaster is a multi-layer system applied wet over a lath base. The first coat (scratch coat) is pressed through wood lath strips or metal mesh to create a mechanical bond. A second coat (brown coat) builds thickness. The final finish coat is troweled smooth. Total thickness ranges from 3/4-inch to over 1 inch. Plaster is harder, denser, and far more resistant to dents than drywall — but it’s also more brittle and prone to cracking over time as the structure settles.

How to Tell Which One You Have

1. Tap and Listen

Knock firmly with your knuckle across multiple areas of the wall. Drywall produces a hollow, resonant thud with slight flex. Plaster sounds dense, solid, and acoustically dead — like tapping stone. Textured paint or wallpaper can mask the sound, so test in several spots.

2. Analyze Crack Patterns

Plaster develops fine, irregular spiderweb cracks that spread across field areas due to lath movement or foundation settling. Drywall cracks are typically straight, hairline fractures confined to taped seams, inside corners, or above doors and windows. Wide diagonal cracks near openings may indicate structural issues regardless of material.

3. Inspect Behind an Outlet Cover (Safely)

Turn off the circuit breaker for that outlet. Verify power is OFF using a non-contact voltage tester before touching any screws. Remove the cover plate and shine a flashlight into the gap beside the electrical box. Drywall shows a uniform gypsum core with paper facing and clean-cut edges. Plaster reveals a multi-layer system with visible trowel marks or lath shadows behind the finish coat, often totaling 3/4-inch to 1-inch thick.

4. Test Surface Hardness

Press a blunt object — like the handle of a screwdriver — firmly against an inconspicuous area. Drywall dents readily with moderate pressure. Plaster resists indentation and feels rock-hard. Avoid sharp tools that could damage historic finishes.

5. Cross-Reference Construction Era (With Caveats)

  • Pre-1940: Almost certainly full plaster on wood lath.
  • 1940–1970: Transition period. Many homes mix materials — plaster in formal rooms, drywall in additions and basements — or use hybrid systems like gypsum lath with plaster veneer. Do not assume uniformity.
  • Post-1970: Predominantly drywall, though some high-end custom builds retained plaster.

Always verify physically per room. Era alone is not definitive.

6. Document Findings Per Room

Photograph outlet edge views, note crack types, and record the material type by location. Repair products are not interchangeable: use setting-type joint compound or lime-based patching plaster for plaster walls, standard drywall mud for drywall. Mismatched repairs fail prematurely.

Advanced Identification Methods

  • Backside Inspection: In unfinished attics, basements, or closets, view the wall from the rear. Drywall has manufacturer printing, tapered edges, and seam tape. Plaster shows wood lath strips (about 1 1/4-inch wide with gaps) or diamond-mesh metal lath with rust staining.
  • Fastener Clues: Exposed nail heads or removed trim reveal fastener types. Square-cut nails or rose-head nails indicate the plaster era. Flat-head drywall nails or Phillips-drive screws confirm drywall.
  • Dust Texture Test: When drilling a small test hole in a hidden area, collect the dust on dark paper. Drywall dust is ultra-fine, white, and talc-smooth. Traditional plaster dust is coarser, gritty, and ranges from off-white to light gray depending on the aggregate. Modern veneer plaster may mimic drywall dust — combine this test with others.

Pro Tips

Tip: When in Doubt, Assume Plaster in Older Homes: If your house predates 1970 and identification is ambiguous, treat walls as plaster until proven otherwise. Using drywall mud on plaster causes adhesion failure and cracking. Setting-type compounds like Durabond bond reliably to both substrates and are safer defaults for uncertain situations.

Caution: Electrical Safety Is Non-Negotiable: Never skip the voltage tester step when removing outlet covers. Breakers can be mislabeled, backfed, or faulty. If the tester indicates voltage after shutoff, stop immediately and consult an electrician. Outlet boxes in plaster walls are often embedded deeper and surrounded by crumbling material — handle gently to avoid damaging historic fabric or exposing live conductors.
