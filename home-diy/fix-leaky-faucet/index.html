Fix a Leaky Faucet: Cartridge, Compression, or Ball Type

June 11, 2026

A dripping faucet isn’t just annoying—it can waste hundreds of gallons of water a year. The fix depends entirely on what type of valve is inside your faucet, and the most common mistake is buying a generic repair kit that almost fits. I once rebuilt a bathroom faucet with a universal kit only to have it start dripping again two weeks later because the replacement cartridge was a millimeter shorter than the original. Now I always look up the exact model number before buying parts. This guide covers the four main faucet types and how to fix each one correctly.

What You’ll Need

  • Phillips screwdriver
  • Flathead screwdriver
  • Adjustable wrench
  • Needle-nose pliers
  • Drain stopper or clean rag (critical for preventing lost parts)
  • Exact replacement parts (verified by faucet model number—not generic size)
  • Plumber’s silicone grease (not petroleum-based lubricants)
  • White vinegar
  • Clean lint-free rags

Steps

  1. Shut off the water and protect the drain. Close both hot and cold shut-off valves beneath the sink completely. Open the faucet to release residual pressure and confirm water flow has stopped. Immediately insert a drain stopper or tightly folded rag into the sink opening. This single precaution prevents screws, washers, and springs from disappearing down the drain during disassembly—a mistake that turns a simple repair into a plumbing rescue mission.
  2. Remove the handle and identify the valve type. Pry off the decorative cap, often marked “H” or “C,” with a flathead screwdriver. Remove the exposed handle screw and lift the handle straight upward without twisting. Examine the internal mechanism carefully. Compression valves show a threaded brass stem with a visible rubber washer at the base. Cartridge types feature a smooth, sealed cylindrical body. Ball valves display a hollow sphere with lateral slots. Ceramic disc systems contain two stacked, polished plates with matching cutouts. Misidentifying your valve type leads to incorrect part purchases.
  3. Document the configuration before disassembly. Take clear photographs of every component’s orientation, alignment tabs, spring placement, and wear patterns before removing anything. Locate and record the manufacturer name and model number stamped on the valve body, escutcheon plate, or original packaging. Universal “fits-most” kits frequently fail due to millimeter-scale differences in stem length, spline count, or seal diameter.
  4. Extract worn components safely. Use an adjustable wrench to loosen the retaining nut counterclockwise with steady pressure. If the cartridge or stem resists removal, soak the assembly in undiluted white vinegar for 30 minutes to dissolve mineral scale. Never force extraction with metal prying tools that gouge precision-machined valve seats. Once loosened, pull the component straight out using needle-nose pliers gripping non-sealing surfaces only.
  5. Replace type-specific sealing elements. Match replacements exactly to your documented valve type. For compression faucets, replace both the rubber washer and the stem O-ring. For cartridge or ceramic disc units, install only OEM-specified replacements—aftermarket substitutes rarely match factory tolerances. Ball-type repairs require new cam washers, tension springs, and inlet and outlet rubber seats. Coat all rubber seals lightly with plumber’s silicone grease. Petroleum-based products degrade elastomers over time.
  6. Reassemble with the correct torque sequence. Reinstall components in reverse order using your reference photos to ensure proper alignment. Hand-start all threaded connections first to prevent cross-threading. Tighten the retaining nut only until rotational play disappears. Excessive torque cracks plastic valve bodies or fractures ceramic discs. Never use threadlocker on faucet internals unless specified by the manufacturer.
  7. Restore the water and verify leak-free operation. Slowly reopen the shut-off valves while watching all joints for immediate leakage. Run the faucet through full hot-to-cold travel for 60 seconds to flush debris and allow seals to seat fully. Inspect the spout aerator, handle base, and supply line connections under bright light. Initial minor seepage often resolves within minutes as gaskets compress. Continuous dripping indicates misalignment, a wrong part, or a damaged valve seat requiring professional assessment.

Pro Tips

Tip: Before purchasing parts, search the faucet’s model number plus “repair kit” on the manufacturer’s website. Brands like Moen, Delta, and Kohler offer application-specific kits with guaranteed compatibility. Hardware store assortments save time upfront but cost more long-term when incorrect seals cause repeat failures.

Caution: Never attempt a faucet repair without shutting off both water supplies, even if only one side is leaking. Cross-connected plumbing can backfeed unexpectedly. Also avoid using WD-40 or similar penetrants on valve internals. They leave residues that attract grit and accelerate seal degradation. Stick to vinegar for descaling and silicone grease for lubrication.
